Is IT asset management a boring routine or a creative task?

While thinking about IT asset management, I remembered a case study. An IT capacity manager in a large company periodically prepared a thick report to the management. Once again, he did not bring a report, deciding to check whether it was needed at all. After waiting a few days, he came to the manager and asked: “It seems that the report that I am preparing is not particularly in demand, but what do you really need?” The manager admitted that the massive report is good, but it is not used. And I sketched on a piece of paper what I would like to have at hand.

IT asset management, like configuration management, is a basic process for others. In the first case, the financial aspect is more important, in the second, operational tasks. Both processes can be overloaded with details and turned into something routine. How to find a balance and make the process truly "alive"?

Of course, the balance of unification and personal approach is everyone's business. Recall the story of the post-war automotive industry: American and Japanese tactics. Gradually, the Japanese achieved superiority thanks, among other things, to a well-balanced balance of unification and production culture. But this rule is not for everyone.
